Bo\xc3\xb6tes /bo\xca\x8a\xcb\x88o\xca\x8ati\xcb\x90z/ is a constellation in the northern sky, located between 0\xc2\xb0 and +60\xc2\xb0 declination, and 13 and 16 hours of right ascension on the celestial sphere. The name comes from the Greek \xce\x92\xce\xbf\xcf\x8e\xcf\x84\xce\xb7\xcf\x82, Bo\xc5\x8dt\xc4\x93s, meaning herdsman or plowman (literally, ox-driver; from boos, related to the Latin bovis, \xe2\x80\x9ccow\xe2\x80\x9d). The "\xc3\xb6" in the name is a diaeresis, not an umlaut, meaning that each \'o\' is to be pronounced separately.

\n

Bo\xc3\xb6tes was one of the 48 constellations described by the 2nd century astronomer Ptolemy and is now one of the 88 modern constellations. It contains the fourth brightest star in the night sky, Arcturus. Bo\xc3\xb6tes is home to many other bright stars, including eight above the fourth magnitude and an additional 21 above the fifth magnitude, making a total of 29 stars easily visible to the naked eye.

History and mythology[edit]

\n

In ancient Babylon the stars of Bo\xc3\xb6tes were known as SHU.PA. They were apparently depicted as the god Enlil, who was the leader of the Babylonian pantheon and special patron of farmers.[3]

\n

The name Bo\xc3\xb6tes was first used by Homer in his Odyssey as a celestial reference point for navigation,[4] described as "late-setting" or "slow to set", translated as the "Plowman".[5] Exactly whom Bo\xc3\xb6tes is supposed to represent in Greek mythology is not clear. According to one version, he was a son of Demeter,[6] Philomenus, twin brother of Plutus, a ploughman who drove the oxen in the constellation Ursa Major.[7] This is corroborated by the constellation\'s name, which itself means "ox-driver" or "herdsman."[8] The ancient Greeks saw the asterism now called the "Big Dipper" or "Plough" as a cart with oxen.[1] This influenced the name\'s etymology, derived from the Greek for "noisy" or "ox-driver".[9] Another myth associated with Bo\xc3\xb6tes tells that he invented the plow and was memorialized for his ingenuity as a constellation.[10]

\n

Another myth associated with Bo\xc3\xb6tes by Hyginus is that of Icarius, who was schooled as a grape farmer and winemaker by Dionysus. Icarius made wine so strong that those who drank it appeared poisoned, which caused shepherds to avenge their supposedly poisoned friends by killing Icarius. Maera, Icarius\'s dog, brought his daughter Erigone to her father\'s body, whereupon both she and the dog committed suicide. Zeus then chose to honor all three by placing them in the sky as constellations: Icarius as Bo\xc3\xb6tes, Erigone as Virgo, and Maera as Canis Major or Canis Minor.[9]

\n

Following another reading, the constellation is identified with Arcas and also referred to as Arcas and Arcturus, son of Zeus and Callisto. Arcas was brought up by his maternal grandfather Lycaon, to whom one day Zeus went and had a meal. To verify that the guest was really the king of the gods, Lycaon killed his grandson and prepared a meal made from his flesh. Zeus noticed and became very angry, transforming Lycaon into a wolf and gave back life to his son. In the meantime Callisto had been transformed into a she-bear, by Zeus\' wife, Hera, who was angry at Zeus\' infidelity.[7][9] This is corroborated by the Greek name for Bo\xc3\xb6tes, Arctophylax, which means "Bear Watcher".[9] Callisto in form of a bear was almost killed by her son who was out hunting. Zeus rescued her, taking her into the sky where she became Ursa Major, "the Great Bear". The name Arcturus (the constellation\'s brightest star) comes from the Greek word meaning "guardian of the bear". Sometimes Arcturus is depicted as leading the hunting dogs of nearby Canes Venatici and driving the bears of Ursa Major and Ursa Minor.[11]

Several former constellations were formed from stars now included in Bo\xc3\xb6tes. Quadrans Muralis, the Quadrant, was a constellation created near Beta Bo\xc3\xb6tis from faint stars.[12] It was invented in 1795 by J\xc3\xa9r\xc3\xb4me Lalande, an astronomer who used a quadrant to perform detailed astronometric measurements. Lalande worked with Nicole-Reine Lepaute and others to predict the 1758 return of Halley\'s Comet. Quadrans Muralis was formed from the stars of eastern Bo\xc3\xb6tes, western Hercules, and Draco.[13] It was originally called Le Mural by Jean Fortin in his 1795 Atlas C\xc3\xa9leste; it was not given the name Quadrans Muralis until Johann Bode\'s 1801 Uranographia. The constellation was quite faint, with its brightest stars reaching the 5th magnitude.[14] Mons Maenalus, representing the Maenalus mountains, was created by Johannes Hevelius in 1687 at the foot of the constellation\'s figure. The mountain was named for the son of Lycaon, Maenalus. The mountain, one of Diana\'s hunting grounds, was also holy to Pan.[15]

Non-Western astronomy[edit]

\n

The stars of Bo\xc3\xb6tes were incorporated into many different Chinese constellations. Arcturus was part of the most prominent of these, variously designated as the celestial king\'s throne (Tian Wang) or the Blue Dragon\'s horn (Daijiao); the name Daijiao, meaning "great horn", is more common. Arcturus was given such importance in Chinese celestial mythology because of its status marking the beginning of the lunar calendar, as well as its status as the brightest star in the northern night sky. Two constellations flanked Daijiao, Yousheti to the right and Zuosheti to the left; they represented companions that orchestrated the seasons. Zuosheti was formed from modern Zeta, Omicron, and Pi Bo\xc3\xb6tis, while Yousheti was formed from modern Eta, Tau, and Upsilon Bo\xc3\xb6tis. Dixi, the Emperor\'s ceremonial banquet mat, was north of Arcturus, consisting of the stars 12, 11, and 9 Bo\xc3\xb6tis. Another northern constellation was Qigong, the Seven Dukes, which was mostly across the Bo\xc3\xb6tes-Hercules border. It included either Delta Bo\xc3\xb6tis or Beta Bo\xc3\xb6tis as its terminus.[9]

\n

The other Chinese constellations made up of the stars of Bo\xc3\xb6tes existed in the modern constellation\'s north; they are all representations of weapons. Tianqiang, the spear, was formed from Iota, Kappa, and Theta Bo\xc3\xb6tis; Genghe, variously representing a lance or shield, was formed from Epsilon, Rho, and Sigma Bo\xc3\xb6tis. There were also two weapons made up of a singular star. Xuange, the halberd, was represented by Lambda Bo\xc3\xb6tis, and Zhaoyao, either the sword or the spear, was represented by Gamma Bo\xc3\xb6tis.[9]

\n

Two Chinese constellations have an uncertain placement in Bo\xc3\xb6tes. Kangchi, the lake, was placed south of Arcturus, though its specific location is disputed. It may have been placed entirely in Bo\xc3\xb6tes, on either side of the Bo\xc3\xb6tes-Virgo border, or on either side of the Virgo-Libra border. The constellation Zhouding, a bronze tripod-mounted container used for food, was sometimes cited as the stars 1, 2, and 6 Bo\xc3\xb6tis. However, it has also been associated with three stars in Coma Berenices.[9]

\n

Antares had several names that described its significance to indigenous Polynesians. In the Society Islands, Arcturus, called Ana-tahua-taata-metua-te-tupu-mavae ("a pillar to stand by"), was one of the ten "pillars of the sky", bright stars that represented the ten heavens of the Tahitian afterlife.[16] In Hawaii, the pattern of Bo\xc3\xb6tes was called Hoku-iwa, meaning "stars of the frigate bird". This constellation marked the path for Hawaiiloa on his return to Hawaii from the South Pacific Ocean.[17] The Hawaiians called Arcturus Hoku-lea.[18] It was equated to the Tuamotuan constellation Te Kiva, meaning "frigate-bird", which could either represent the figure of Bo\xc3\xb6tes or just Arcturus.[19] However, Arcturus may instead be the Tuamotuan star called Turu.[20] The Hawaiian name for Arcturus as a single star was likely Hoku-lea, which means "star of gladness".[21] In the Marquesas Islands, Arcturus was probably called Tau-tou and was the star that ruled the month approximating January. The Maori and Moriori called it Tautoru, a variant of the Marquesan name and a name shared with Orion\'s Belt.[22]

Characteristics[edit]

\n

Bo\xc3\xb6tes is a constellation bordered by Virgo to the south, Coma Berenices and Canes Venatici to the west, Ursa Major to the northwest, Draco to the northeast, and Hercules, Corona Borealis and Serpens Caput to the east. The three-letter abbreviation for the constellation, as adopted by the International Astronomical Union in 1922, is \'Boo\'.[23] The official constellation boundaries, as set by Eug\xc3\xa8ne Delporte in 1930, are defined by a polygon of 16 segments. In the equatorial coordinate system, the right ascension coordinates of these borders lie between 13h 36.1m and 15h 49.3m, while the declination coordinates stretch from +7.36\xc2\xb0 to +55.1\xc2\xb0.[2] Covering 907 square degrees, Bo\xc3\xb6tes culminates at midnight around 2 May and ranks 13th in area.[24]

\n

Colloquially, its pattern of stars has been likened to a kite or ice cream cone.[24][25] However, depictions of Bo\xc3\xb6tes have varied historically. Aratus described him circling the north pole, herding the two bears. Later ancient Greek depictions, described by Ptolemy, have him holding the reins of his hunting dogs (Canes Venatici) in his left hand, with a spear, club, or staff in his right hand.[9] After Hevelius introduced Mons Maenalus in 1681, Bo\xc3\xb6tes was often depicted standing on the Peloponnese mountain.[15] By 1801, when Johann Bode published his Uranographia, Bo\xc3\xb6tes had acquired a sickle, which was also held in his left hand.[9]

\n

The placement of Arcturus has also been mutable through the centuries. Traditionally, Arcturus lay between his thighs, as Ptolemy depicted him. However, Germanicus Caesar deviated from this tradition by placing Arcturus "where his garment is fastened by a knot".[9]

Stars[edit]

\n
See also: List of stars in Bo\xc3\xb6tes
\n

In his Uranometria, Johann Bayer used the Greek letters Alpha through to Omega and then A to k to label what he saw as the most prominent 35 stars in the constellation, with subsequent astronomers splitting Kappa, Mu, Nu and Pi as two stars each. Nu is also the same star as Psi Herculis.[26] John Flamsteed numbered 54 stars for the constellation.[27]

\n

Arcturus, or Alpha Bo\xc3\xb6tis, is the brightest star in Bo\xc3\xb6tes and the fourth brightest star in the sky at an apparent magnitude of \xe2\x88\x920.04; it has an absolute magnitude of \xe2\x88\x920.2. It is also the brightest star north of the celestial equator, barely brighter than Vega and Capella.[12][28] Its name comes from the Greek for "bear-keeper". An orange giant with color visible from Earth at a distance of 37 light-years, its diameter is 27 solar diameters,[7] equivalent to approximately 32 million kilometers,[12] though its mass is approximately one solar mass.[7] Its luminosity is 115 L\xe2\x98\x89 and its spectral class is K2.[28] Bayer located Arcturus above the herdsman\'s left knee in his Uranometria.[29]

\n

Marking the herdsman\'s head is Beta Bo\xc3\xb6tis,[29] or Nekkar, a yellow giant of magnitude 3.5. Its common name comes from the Arabic phrase for "ox-driver". It is 219 light-years away[7] and has a luminosity of 58 L\xe2\x98\x89. Its absolute magnitude is 0.3 and its spectral class is G8.[12] Eta Bo\xc3\xb6tis or Muphrid is a 2.68 magnitude star that is 37 light-years away with a spectral class of G0.[28] It has a luminosity of 6.5 L\xe2\x98\x89.[12] It is the uppermost star denoting the herdsman\'s left leg.[29] Muphrid and Arcturus lie only 3.3 light years away from each other. Viewed from Arcturus, Muphrid would have a visual magnitude of -2\xc2\xbd, while Arcturus would be around visual magnitude -4\xc2\xbd when seen from Muphrid.[30] Gamma Bo\xc3\xb6tis, or Seginus, is a white star of magnitude 3.03. It is 85 light-years away.[7] Its spectral class is A7,[28] and it has a luminosity of 53 L\xe2\x98\x89.[12] Rho and Sigma Bo\xc3\xb6tis denote the herdsman\'s waist.[29] With a visual magnitude of 3.58, Rho has a luminosity of 105 L\xe2\x98\x89 and is 183 light-years from Earth. Sigma is of magnitude 4.46, with a spectral class of F2 and an absolute magnitude of 3.0. The magnitude 4.05 Theta Bo\xc3\xb6tis has a spectral type of F7 and an absolute magnitude of 3.8. There are two dimmer F-type stars, magnitude 4.83 12 Bo\xc3\xb6tis, class F8; and magnitude 4.93 45 Bo\xc3\xb6tis, class F5.[12] Xi Bo\xc3\xb6tis is a G8 yellow dwarf of magnitude 4.55, and absolute magnitude is 5.5. Two dimmer G-type stars are magnitude 4.86 31 Bo\xc3\xb6tis, class G8, and magnitude 4.76 44 Bo\xc3\xb6tis, class G0.[12]

\n

Of apparent magnitude 4.06, Upsilon Bo\xc3\xb6tis has a spectral class of K5 and an absolute magnitude of \xe2\x88\x920.3. Dimmer than Upsilon Bo\xc3\xb6tis is magnitude 4.54 Phi Bo\xc3\xb6tis, with a spectral class of K2 and an absolute magnitude of \xe2\x88\x920.1. Just slightly dimmer than Phi at magnitude 4.60 is O Bo\xc3\xb6tis, which, like Izar, has a spectral class of K0. O Bo\xc3\xb6tis has an absolute magnitude of 0.2. The other four dim stars are magnitude 4.91 6 Bo\xc3\xb6tis, class K4; magnitude 4.86 20 Bo\xc3\xb6tis, class K3; magnitude 4.81 Omega Bo\xc3\xb6tis, class K4; and magnitude 4.83 A Bo\xc3\xb6tis, class K1.[12]

\n

There is one bright B-class star in Bo\xc3\xb6tes; magnitude 4.93 Pi1 Bo\xc3\xb6tis, also called Alazal. It has a spectral class of B9 and is 40 parsecs from Earth. There is also one M-type star, magnitude 4.81 34 Bo\xc3\xb6tis. It is of class gM0.[12]

Epsilon Bo\xc3\xb6tis, also known as Izar or Pulcherrima, is a close triple star popular with amateur astronomers and the most prominent binary star in Bo\xc3\xb6tes. The primary is a yellow-[31] or orange-hued magnitude 2.5 giant star, the secondary is a magnitude 4.6 blue-hued main-sequence star,[7] and the tertiary is a magnitude 12.0 star.[31] The system is 210 light-years away. The name "Izar" comes from the Arabic word for "girdle" or "loincloth", referring to its location in the constellation. The name "Pulcherrima" comes from the Latin phrase for "most beautiful", referring to its contrasting colors in a telescope.[7] The primary and secondary stars are separated by 2.9 arcseconds at an angle of 341 degrees; the primary\'s spectral class is K0 and it has a luminosity of 200 L\xe2\x98\x89.[12][31] To the naked eye, Izar has a magnitude of 2.37.[12]

\n

Mu Bo\xc3\xb6tis, known as Alkalurops, is a triple star popular with amateur astronomers. It has an overall magnitude of 4.3 and is 121 light-years away. Its name is from the Arabic phrase for "club" or "staff". The primary appears to be of magnitude 4.3 and is blue-white. The secondary appears to be of magnitude 6.5, but is actually a close double star itself with a primary of magnitude 7.0 and a secondary of magnitude 7.6. The secondary and tertiary stars have an orbital period of 260 years.[7] The primary has an absolute magnitude of 2.6 and is of spectral class F0.[12] The secondary and tertiary stars are separated by 2 arcseconds; the primary and secondary are separated by 109.1 arcseconds at an angle of 171 degrees.[32]

\n

The two components are separated by 1.0 arcseconds at an angle of 303 degrees. The optical companion is of magnitude 10.9, separated by 99.3 arcseconds at an angle of 259 degrees. 44 Bo\xc3\xb6tis is an eclipsing variable star. The primary is of variable magnitude and the secondary is of magnitude 6.2; they have an orbital period of 225 years. The components are separated by 1.0 arcseconds at an angle of 40 degrees.[12]

Variable stars[edit]

\n

Bo\xc3\xb6tes possesses several variable stars. T Bo\xc3\xb6tis was a nova observed in April 1860 at a magnitude of 9.7. It has never been observed since, but that does not preclude the possibility of it being a highly irregular variable star or a recurrent nova.[28]

\n

Two of the brighter Mira-type variables stars in the constellations are R Bo\xc3\xb6tis and S Bo\xc3\xb6tis. R Bo\xc3\xb6tis is an M-type star that ranges in magnitude from a minimum of 13.1 to a maximum of 6.2 over a period of 223.4 days.[33][12] S Bo\xc3\xb6tis is another M-type Mira variable that ranges in magnitude from a minimum of 13.8 to a maximum of 7.8 over a period of 270.7 days.[12]

\n

44 Bo\xc3\xb6tis (i Bo\xc3\xb6tis) is a double variable star 42 light-years away. It has an overall magnitude of 4.8 and appears yellow to the naked eye. The primary is of magnitude 5.3 and the secondary is of magnitude 6.1; their orbital period is 220 years. The secondary is itself an eclipsing variable star with a range of 0.6 magnitudes; its orbital period is 6.4 hours.[7] It is a W Ursae Majoris variable that ranges in magnitude from a minimum of 7.1 to a maximum of 6.5 every 0.27 days. Both stars are G-type stars. Another W UMa-type star is ZZ Bo\xc3\xb6tis, which has two G2-type components and ranges in magnitude from a minimum of 6.4 to a maximum of 5.8. It has a period of 5.0 days.[12]

\n

Bo\xc3\xb6tes has two semi-regular variable stars. V Bo\xc3\xb6tis is an M-type star that ranges in magnitude from a minimum of 12.0 to a maximum of 7.0, over a period of 258 days. W Bo\xc3\xb6tis is a brighter M-type star; it has a minimum magnitude of 5.4 and a maximum magnitude of 4.7, with a period of 450 days.[12]

\n

BL Bo\xc3\xb6tis is the prototype of its class of pulsating variable stars,[34] the anomalous Cepheids. These stars are somewhat similar to Cepheid variables, but they do not have the same relationship between their period and luminosity.[35] Their periods are similar to RRAB variables; however, they are far brighter than these stars.[36] BL Bo\xc3\xb6tis is a member of the cluster NGC 5466. Anomalous Cepheids are metal poor and have masses not much larger than the Sun\'s, on average, 1.5 solar masses. BL Bo\xc3\xb6tis type stars are a subtype of RR Lyrae variables.[37]

\n

An apparent magnitude 4.18 type A0p star,[12] Lambda Bo\xc3\xb6tis is the prototype of a class of chemically peculiar stars, only some of which pulsate as Delta Scuti type stars. The distinction between the Lambda Bo\xc3\xb6tis stars as a class of stars with peculiar spectra, and the delta Scuti stars whose class describes pulsation in low-overtone pressure modes, is an important one. While many Lambda Bo\xc3\xb6tis stars pulsate and are delta Scuti stars, not many delta Scuti stars have Lambda Bo\xc3\xb6tis peculiarities, since the Lambda Bo\xc3\xb6tis stars are a much rarer class whose members can be found both inside and outside the delta Scuti instability strip. Lambda Bo\xc3\xb6tis stars are dwarf stars that can be either spectral class A or F.[38] Like BL Bo\xc3\xb6tis-type stars they are metal-poor.[39] Scientists have had difficulty explaining the characteristics of Lambda Bo\xc3\xb6tis stars, partly because only around 60 confirmed members exist, but also due to heterogeneity in the literature. Lambda has an absolute magnitude of 1.8.[12]

Stars with planetary systems[edit]

\n

Extrasolar planets have been discovered encircling ten stars in Bo\xc3\xb6tes as of 2012. Tau Bo\xc3\xb6tis is orbited by a large planet, discovered in 1999. The host star itself is a magnitude 4.5 star of type F7V, 15.6 parsecs from Earth. It has a mass of 1.3 solar masses and a radius of 1.331 solar radii; a companion, GJ527B, orbits at a distance of 240 AU. Tau Bo\xc3\xb6tis b, the sole planet discovered in the system, orbits at a distance of 0.046 AU every 3.31 days. Discovered through radial velocity measurements, it has a mass of 5.95 Jupiter masses.[40] This makes it a hot Jupiter.[41] The host star and planet are tidally locked, meaning that the planet\'s orbit and the star\'s particularly high rotation are synchronized.[42][43] Furthermore, a slight variability in the host star\'s light may be caused by magnetic interactions with the planet.[43] Carbon monoxide is present in the planet\'s atmosphere. Tau Bo\xc3\xb6tis b does not transit its star, rather, its orbit is inclined 46 degrees.[41] Like Tau Bo\xc3\xb6tis b, HAT-P-4 b is also a hot Jupiter. It is noted for orbiting a particularly metal-rich host star and being of low density.[44] Discovered in 2007, HAT-P-4 b has a mass of 0.68 Jupiter masses and a radius of 1.27 Jupiter radii. It orbits every 3.05 days at a distance of 0.04 AU. HAT-P-4, the host star, is an F-type star of magnitude 11.2, 310 parsecs from Earth. It is larger than the Sun, with a mass of 1.26 solar masses and a radius of 1.59 solar radii.[45]

Bo\xc3\xb6tes is also home to multiple-planet systems. HD 128311 is the host star for a two-planet system, consisting of HD 128311 b and HD 128311 c, discovered in 2002 and 2005, respectively.[46][47] HD 128311 b is the smaller planet, with a mass of 2.18 Jupiter masses; it was discovered through radial velocity observations. It orbits at almost the same distance as Earth, at 1.099 AU; however, its orbital period is significantly longer at 448.6 days.[46] The larger of the two, HD 128311 c, has a mass of 3.21 Jupiter masses and was discovered in the same manner. It orbits every 919 days inclined at 50\xc2\xb0, and is 1.76 AU from the host star.[47] The host star, HD 128311, is a K0V-type star located 16.6 parsecs from Earth. It is smaller than the Sun, with a mass of 0.84 solar masses and a radius of 0.73 solar radii; it also appears below the threshold of naked-eye visibility at an apparent magnitude of 7.51.[46]

\n

There are several single-planet systems in Bo\xc3\xb6tes. HD 132406 is a sun-like star of spectral type G0V with an apparent magnitude of 8.45, 231.5 light-years from Earth.[48] It has a mass of 1.09 solar masses and a radius of 1 solar radius.[49] The star is orbited by a gas giant, HD 132406 b, discovered in 2007.[48] HD 132406 orbits 1.98 AU from its host star with a period of 974 days and has a mass of 5.61 Jupiter masses. The planet was discovered by the radial velocity method.[49] WASP-23 is a star with one orbiting planet, WASP-23 b. The planet, discovered by the transit method in 2010, orbits every 2.944 very close to its sun, at 0.0376 AU. It is smaller than Jupiter, at 0.884 Jupiter masses and 0.962 Jupiter radii. Its star is a K1V type star of apparent magnitude 12.7, far below naked-eye visibility, and smaller than the Sun at 0.78 solar masses and 0.765 solar radii.[50] HD 131496 is also encircled by one planet, HD 131496 b. The star is of type K0 and is located 110 parsecs from Earth; it appears at a visual magnitude of 7.96. It is significantly larger than the Sun, with a mass of 1.61 solar masses and a radius of 4.6 solar radii. Its one planet, discovered in 2011 by the radial velocity method, has a mass of 2.2 Jupiter masses; its radius is as yet undetermined. HD 131496 b orbits at a distance of 2.09 AU with a period of 883 days.[51]

\n

Another single planetary system in Bo\xc3\xb6tes is the HD 132563 system, a triple star system. The parent star, technically HD 132563B, is a star of magnitude 9.47, 96 parsecs from Earth. It is almost exactly the size of the sun, with the same radius and a mass only 1% greater. Its planet, HD 132563B b, was discovered in 2011 by the radial velocity method. 1.49 times the mass of Jupiter, it orbits 2.62 AU from its star with a period of 1544 days.[52] Its orbit is somewhat elliptical, with an eccentricity of 0.22. HD 132563B b is one of very few planets found in triple star systems; it orbits the isolated member of the system, which is separated from the other components, a spectroscopic binary, by 400 AU.[53] Also discovered through the radial velocity method, albeit a year earlier, is HD 136418 b, a 2-Jupiter mass planet that orbits the star HD 136418 at a distance of 1.32 AU with a period of 464.3 days. Its host star is a magnitude 7.88 G5-type star, 98.2 parsecs from Earth. It has a radius of 3.4 solar radii and a mass of 1.33 solar masses.[54]

\n

WASP-14 b is one of the most massive and dense exoplanets known,[55] with a mass of 7.341 Jupiter masses and a radius of 1.281 Jupiter radii. Discovered via the transit method, it orbits 0.036 AU from its host star with a period of 2.24 days.[56] WASP-14 b has a density of 4.6 grams per cubic centimeter, making it one of the densest exoplanets known.[55] Its host star, WASP-14, is an F5V-type star of magnitude 9.75, 160 parsecs from Earth. It has a radius of 1.306 solar radii and a mass of 1.211 solar masses.[56] It also has a very high proportion of lithium.[55]

Deep-sky objects[edit]

\n

Bo\xc3\xb6tes is in a part of the celestial sphere facing away from the plane of our home Milky Way galaxy, and so does not have open clusters or nebulae. Instead, it has one bright globular cluster and many faint galaxies.[1] The globular cluster NGC 5466 has an overall magnitude of 9.1 and a diameter of 11 arcminutes.[12] It is a very loose globular cluster with fairly few stars and may appear as a rich, concentrated open cluster in a telescope. NGC 5466 is classified as a Shapley-Sawyer Concentration Class 12 cluster, reflecting its sparsity.[57] Its fairly large diameter means that it has a low surface brightness, so it appears far dimmer than the catalogued magnitude of 9.1 and requires a large amateur telescope to view. Only approximately 12 stars are resolved by an amateur instrument.[58]

\n

Bo\xc3\xb6tes has two bright galaxies. NGC 5248 (Caldwell 45) is a type Sc galaxy (a variety of spiral galaxy) of magnitude 10.2. It measures 6.5 by 4.9 arcminutes.[12] 50 million light-years from Earth, NGC 5248 is a member of the Virgo Cluster of galaxies; it has dim outer arms and obvious H II regions, dust lanes, and young star clusters.[59] NGC 5676 is another type Sc galaxy of magnitude 10.9. It measures 3.9 by 2.0 arcminutes.[12] Other galaxies include NGC 5008, a type Sc emission-line galaxy,[60] NGC 5548, a type S Seyfert galaxy,[61] NGC 5653, a type S HII galaxy,[62] NGC 5778 (also classified as NGC 5825),[63] a type E galaxy that is the brightest of its cluster,[64] NGC 5886,[65] and NGC 5888, a type SBb galaxy.[66] NGC 5698 is a barred spiral galaxy, notable for being the host of the 2005 supernova SN 2005bc, which peaked at magnitude 15.3.

\n

Much further away lies the 250 million light-year diameter Bo\xc3\xb6tes void, a huge space largely empty of galaxies. Its centre is roughly 700 million light years from Earth. Beyond it and within the bounds of the constellation, lie two superclusters at around 830 million and 1 billion light years distant.

Meteor showers[edit]

\n
\n
\n
\n
\nA Quadrantid captured by an all-sky camera during a 4-second exposure.
\n
\n
\n

Bo\xc3\xb6tes is home to the Quadrantid meteor shower, the most prolific annual meteor shower. It was discovered in January 1835 and named in 1864 by Alexander Hershell.[67] The radiant is located in northern Bo\xc3\xb6tes near Kappa Bo\xc3\xb6tis,[68] in its namesake former constellation of Quadrans Muralis. Quadrantid meteors are dim, but have a peak visible hourly rate of approximately 100 per hour on January 3\xe2\x80\x934.[7][28] The zenithal hourly rate of the Quadrantids is approximately 130 meteors per hour at their peak; it is also a very narrow shower. The Quadrantids are notoriously difficult to observe because of a low radiant and often inclement weather. The parent body of the meteor shower has been disputed for decades;[67] however, Peter Jenniskens has proposed 2003 EH1, a minor planet, as the parent.[69] 2003 EH1 may be linked to C/1490 Y1, a comet previously thought to be a potential parent body for the Quadrantids.[70][71] 2003 EH1 is a short-period comet of the Jupiter family; 500 years ago, it experienced a catastrophic breakup event. It is now dormant.[72] The Quadrantids had notable displays in 1982, 1985, and 2004.[73] Meteors from this shower often appear to have a blue hue and travel at a moderate speed of 41.5\xe2\x80\x9343 kilometers per second.[74]

\n

On April 28, 1984, a remarkable outburst of the normally placid Alpha Bootids was observed by visual observer Frank Witte from 00:00 to 2:30 UTC. In a 6 cm telescope, he observed 433 meteors in a field of view near Arcturus with a diameter of less than 1\xc2\xb0. Peter Jenniskens comments that this outburst resembled a "typical dust trail crossing".[75] The Alpha Bootids normally begin on April 14, peaking on April 27th and 28th, and finishing on May 12.[76] Its meteors are slow-moving, with a velocity of 20.9 kilometers per second.[77] They may be related to Comet 73P/Schwassmann-Wachmann 3, but this connection is only theorized.[76]

The June Bootids, also known as the Iota Draconids, is a meteor shower associated with the comet 7P/Pons-Winnecke, first recognized on May 27, 1916 by William F. Denning.[78] The shower, with its slow meteors, was not observed prior to 1916 because Earth did not cross the comet\'s dust trail until Jupiter perturbed Pons-Winnecke\'s orbit, causing it to come within 0.03 AU of Earth\'s orbit the first year the June Bootids were observed. In 1982, E. A. Reznikov discovered that the 1916 outburst was caused by material released from the comet in 1819.[79] Another outburst of the June Bootids was not observed until 1998, because Comet Pons-Winnecke\'s orbit was not in a favorable position. However, on June 27, 1998, an outburst of meteors radiating from Bo\xc3\xb6tes, later confirmed to be associated with Pons-Winnecke, was observed. They were incredibly long-lived, with trails of the brightest meteors lasting several seconds at times. Many fireballs, green-hued trails, and even some meteors that cast shadows were observed throughout the outburst, which had a maximum zenithal hourly rate of 200\xe2\x80\x93300 meteors per hour.[80] In 2002, two Russian astronomers determined that material ejected from the comet in 1825 was responsible for the 1998 outburst.[81] Ejecta from the comet dating to 1819, 1825, and 1830 was predicted to enter Earth\'s atmosphere on June 23, 2004. The predictions of a shower less spectacular than the 1998 showing were borne out in a display that had a maximum zenithal hourly rate of 16\xe2\x80\x9320 meteors per hour that night. The June Bootids are not expected to have another outburst in the next 50 years.[82] Typically, only 1\xe2\x80\x932 dim, very slow meteors are visible per hour; the average June Bootid has a magnitude of 5.0. It is related to the Alpha Draconids and the Bootids-Draconids. The shower lasts from June 27 to July 5, with a peak on the night of June 28.[83] The June Bootids are classified as a class III shower (variable),[84] and has an average entry velocity of 18 kilometers per second. Its radiant is located 7 degrees north of Beta Bo\xc3\xb6tis.[85]

\n

The Beta Bootids is a weak shower that begins on January 5, peaks on January 16, and ends on January 18. Its meteors travel at 43 kilometers/second.[86] The January Bootids is a short, young meteor shower that begins on January 9, peaks from January 16 to January 18, and ends on January 18th.[87] The Phi Bootids is another weak shower radiating from Bo\xc3\xb6tes. It begins on April 16, peaks on April 30 and May 1, and ends on May 12.[76] Its meteors are slow-moving, with a velocity of 15.1 km/s. They were discovered in 2006.[88] The shower\'s peak hourly rate can be as high as 6 meteors per hour. Though named for a star in Bo\xc3\xb6tes, the Phi Bootid radiant has moved into Hercules.[89] The meteor stream is associated with three different asteroids: 1620 Geographos, 2062 Aten, and 1978 CA.[90] The Lambda Bootids, part of the Bootid-Coronae Borealid Complex, are a weak annual shower with moderately fast meteors; 41.75 km/s.[91] The complex includes the Lambda Bootids, as well as the Theta Coronae Borealids and Xi Coronae Borealids.[92][93] All of the Bootid-Coronae Borealid showers are Jupiter family comet showers; the streams in the complex have highly inclined orbits.[72]

\n

There are several minor showers in Bo\xc3\xb6tes, some of whose existence is yet to be verified. The Rho Bootids radiate from near the namesake star, and were hypothesized in 2010.[94] The average Rho Bootid has an entry velocity of 43 km/s.[94][95] It peaks in November and lasts for 3 days. The Rho Bootid shower is part of the SMA complex, a group of meteor showers related to the Taurids, which is in turn linked to the comet 2P/Encke. However, the link to the Taurid shower remains unconfirmed and may be a chance correlation.[95] Another such shower is the Gamma Bootids, which were hypothesized in 2006. Gamma Bootids have an entry velocity of 50.3 km/s.[96] The Nu Bootids, hypothesized in 2012, have faster meteors, with an entry velocity of 62.8 km/s.[97]
